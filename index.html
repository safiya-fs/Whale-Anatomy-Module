Whale Anatomy, Maritime Threats, and Traditional Ecological Knowledge
 What is Traditional Ecological Knowledge? Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) refers to enduring and passed down knowledge, practices, and beliefs that Indigenous peoples and local communities develop through generations of close relationships with the natural environment[1]. TEK includes detailed understanding of ecosystems, animal behaviour, season changes, and sustainable ways of interacting with nature, making it a critical resource in the fight against climate change and the protection of species[1]. TEK views humans, animals, and the environment as part of an interconnected system in which people have responsibilities toward the land and other living beings, unlike many Western scientific approaches that often study nature in isolated parts[1].

When learning about whales, TEK is relevant because many Indigenous and local coastal communities have observed and interacted with whales for centuries[2]. These communities have developed deep knowledge about whales’ behaviour, migration routes, feeding patterns and relationships with marine ecosystems[2]. This knowledge can provide long-term ecological insights, unlike scientific research which often spans only a few decades[2].

Moreover, TEK also emphasizes respect and stewardship toward whales, which establishes them not only as biological species, but as intelligent beings with cultural and spiritual significance[2]. Therefore, incorporating traditional ecological knowledge in sustainability efforts can deepen our understanding of whales and support more holistic approaches to marine conservation.
